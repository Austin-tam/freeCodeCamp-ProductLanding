Types of Cheese


There are many different ways to classify cheese including age, texture, flavour, color, country, type of milk used; while texture is the more common way.
The following is a list of families based on their texture that cheese is commonly categorized under.

Soft cheese, bloomy rind

Soft cheese refers to cheese whose interiors are neither pressed nor cooked. A culture is sprayed onto the cheese during production that gives it the "bloom" when finished. Examples of this type of cheese are Brie, and Camembert.

Soft cheese, washed rind

Cheese with washed rinds have a more dense interior compared to cheeses with bloomy rinds. This is because during the manufacturing process, curdled milk is removed before moulded, whereas for bloomy rinds, the milk is not removed. After ripening, the cheese is "washed" with brine and/or alcohol giving it that smooth exterior finish. Examples include L'Empereur and Champlain.


Semi-soft cheese

Semi soft cheeses have less moisture content than soft cheeses, which results in more physical integrity than soft cheese but not as dense as firm cheese. Semi soft cheeses are good for eating as a snack or can be frozen and cooked without losing flavour. Examples include Cheddar, Mozzarella, Oka, and Monterey Jack.

Firm cheese

Firm cheeses have low moisture content and generally have no rind (outer layer). This is usually due to the cheese being cooked and/or pressed during the manufacturing process.


Blue-veined cheese

Blue-veined cheese, or blue cheese, is similar to soft or uncooked semi-soft cheese. Also similar to the bloomy rinded soft cheese, a culture is used in the manufacturing process. The only difference is that instead of being sprayed on the outside, the culture is mixed in with the curdled milk to allow the mould to develop in the interior of the cheese, resulting in the blueish-green "veins".

Fresh cheese

Fresh cheese is the name given to cheeses that are made without being cooked or aged. They do not have a rind, and are generally soft, creamy, or crumbly.
